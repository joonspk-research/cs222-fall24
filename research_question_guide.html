Appendix: Developing a Research Question: Guidance for the Final Project

Your final project should revolve around a central research question. Here are some suggestions on how to develop a compelling question, with an emphasis on drawing from established social science literature.

1. Replicate a Famous Social Science Study

One approach is to replicate a well-known social science experiment using the generative agents from our class. This allows you to test classic theories or social dynamics in a new context. Examples of studies you could draw inspiration from include:

  • The Milgram Experiment: Simulate how your classmates might respond to authority. How obedient are they in simulated settings?
  • Asch Conformity Experiment: Test how classroom agents conform to group opinions when presented with obvious correct or incorrect choices.
  • Public Goods Games: Investigate cooperative behavior by simulating economic decision-making games like the Public Goods Game or Prisoner’s Dilemma.

2. Explore Group Dynamics

Use the generative agents to examine how small groups interact. For example, you could ask:

  • How do agents collaborate on a group project or task?
  • What types of social roles (e.g., leaders, mediators, dissenters) emerge in group decision-making?
  • How do personality traits (e.g., introversion vs. extroversion) influence group dynamics or the outcomes of a collective task?

3. Investigate Attitudes and Opinion Formation

Design a simulation to explore how classroom agents form or change opinions over time. Some potential ideas include:

  • How do agents respond to persuasive messages or social influence from peers?
  • What factors (e.g., political views, personality traits) drive polarization or consensus within a class discussion?
  • Can we observe any patterns of opinion clusters or “echo chambers” among agents?

4. Simulate Real-World Classroom Scenarios

Create a simulation that mirrors common classroom dynamics. Examples include:

  • How do different teaching methods affect participation and learning among agents?
  • How do agents handle conflict resolution or negotiation during group work?
  • How might the class respond to hypothetical changes in grading or classroom rules?

5. Generate a New Hypothesis Based on Classroom Characteristics

Reflect on the unique makeup of your class. Are there trends or behaviors that seem specific to this group of students? Use those observations to form a research question. Ideas might include:

  • Are there patterns of interaction between agents based on shared traits (e.g., similar academic interests, backgrounds)?
  • How do different personality traits affect responses to hypothetical stressors (e.g., tight deadlines, complex group projects)?

Getting Started: A Step-by-Step Guide

Identify a Known Social Science Study:

Start by reviewing classic studies in social sciences (psychology, sociology, economics). Choose a study you find interesting and consider how it could be replicated or adapted for your classroom agents.

Formulate a Hypothesis or Question:

Once you have a study or idea in mind, ask: “What would happen if this experiment were run using the personalities and dynamics of my classmates?”

  • If you’re replicating a famous study, your hypothesis could be that the agents will behave similarly to the original study’s participants, or you might predict different results due to unique class dynamics.

Design Your Simulation:

Think about how you can structure your simulation to answer your research question. What interactions, surveys, or tasks will the agents perform? How will you collect data from the simulation?

Consult Social Science Literature:

As you design your simulation, refer to relevant social science literature. This will help ground your question in theory and ensure your approach is methodologically sound.

Example Research Questions:

  • Replicating a Public Goods Game: “How do my classmates' agents contribute to a collective resource when given an opportunity to either cooperate or free-ride on others’ contributions?”
  • Group Decision-Making: “In a simulated group task, do agents with higher agreeableness traits emerge as leaders or mediators in resolving conflicts?”
  • Opinion Formation: “How do agents with strong political views influence the opinions of more neutral agents in a class-wide discussion on a controversial topic?”
